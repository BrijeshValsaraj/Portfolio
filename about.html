Curiosity didn't kill the cat - it made a researcher

I didn’t exactly choose research - research chose me. Not in the divine-calling, lightning-strike kind of way, but more like a quiet obsession that grew every time I asked, “Wait... but why?” As a kid, I was that annoying person who read the back of shampoo bottles just to understand what “polyquaternium-10” did and tried (unsuccessfully) to invent new things in the kitchen, often ending in mild chaos and one permanently discolored pressure cooker. But somewhere between accidentally growing mystery fungi in forgotten lunchboxes and dissecting textbooks just to chase one interesting footnote, I realized that science wasn’t just a subject - it was a language, a lens and sometimes, a pretty cool form of mischief.

What truly hooked me, though, was the mess. The glorious, frustrating, head - scratching mess of trying to find patterns in biological systems that often seem to run on their own logic - or none at all. The beauty of research is that no one hands you the answers. You build your own compass, sometimes using duct tape and caffeine, and hope it points to something meaningful. It’s humbling, thrilling and occasionally makes you question all your life choices at 3 a.m. But when something finally clicks, even a small connection, it feels like discovering fire.

I’ve come to see research as equal parts detective work, storytelling, and chaos management. It’s where curiosity meets discipline, and where failure isn’t a dead-end but a sign that you’re asking the right kind of wrong question. As Carl Sagan wisely put it, “Somewhere, something incredible is waiting to be known.” That “somewhere” is what drives me, and that “something” keeps me up at night - in the best way possible.

INTERNSHIPS

TATA INSTITUTE OF FUNDAMENTAL RESEARCH (TIFR)

Hyderabad, India

May 2024 - July 2024

I had the opportunity to intern in the Molecular Physiology Lab under the guidance of Prof. Ullas Kolthur Seetharam at Tata Institute of Fundamental Research Hyderabad.

The lab focuses on uncovering the molecular mechanisms that couple metabolism to cellular and organismal physiology and elucidating processes involved in lifestyle diseases, aging and cancer. Specifically, whether dietary and/or therapeutic interventions reverse or slow down the aging process. The lab aims to identify the tissues that contribute to the aging-induced decline in our functions. Additionally, it also explores whether altered metabolic architecture influences the development of disease states, and how metabolism may control gene expression via epigenetic mechanisms.

During the internship, I worked on two projects - "Investigating the effect of metabolism on alternative splicing" and "Basal characterization of Epithelial cell types across murine Duodenum, Jejunum and Ileum." Here, I worked on cloning of mini genes involved in alternative splicing and determining the expression of epithelial cell type markers across various regions of theintestine based on different feeding conditions.

On the former project I worked on SON-SIRT1 axis - exploring how intron level regulation and acetylation dynamics influence gene expression and splicing under nutrient-sensitive conditions. I had cloned and analyzed ADBRK1 and AKT1, AKT2 to study their potential alternative splicing patterns influenced by SON-SIRT1 activity. On the latter, I analyzed the expression of Muc2, Chromogranin A, Ki67, LGR5 and CD24 under nutrient sensitive conditions.

During this internship, I was able to skills such as RNA Extraction, RTPCR, PCR, Agarose Gel Electrophoresis, qPCR, Cloning and Western Blot. Beyond the technical aspect, we had regular lab meetings and weekly journal club meetings that helped me plan and optimise my experiments, update the team on my progress and gain knowledge on topics related to the field.

BHOPAL MEMORIAL HOSPITAL AND RESEARCH CENTRE (BMHRC)

Bhopal, India

May 2023 - July 2023

I had the opportunity to undertake an internship at Bhopoal Memorial Hospital and Research Centre (BMHRC), Bhopal, India under the guidance of Dr Puneet Gandhi.

The lab works in multi-faceted directions with cross disciplinary approach on varied aspects of medical biotechnology including origin and progression of cancer, molecular regulation of apoptosis, cellular and humoral immune abnormalities, cytogenetic analysis of chromosomal aberrations, micronuclei assay and pathobiology of various diseased states like hepatitis and diabetes.

During the internship I had the opportunity to work on projects that were focused on the combinatorial drug study against the cancerous epithelial cells of the brain and regulation of DNA methylation in type 2 diabetes mellitus patients. Working on these projects, I gained hands on experience on Immunocytochemistry, ELISA, Isolation of DNA from blood, Fluorescence microscopy and Cell Culture. I also had the opportunity to observe and understand how Flow cytometry is important to research.

This internship deepened both my technical expertise and scientific perspective. It taught me the value of collaboration and adaptability, while exposure to lab management and research design helped me understand optimising techniques, ordering necessary equipment and reagents, recording data and other administrative tasks.

ADME and in silico assessment of the immunomodulatory compounds from Phyllodium pulchellum

IMMUNOCON50 Conference, AIIMS New Delhi

October 2023

The study investigates the immunomodulatory potential of phytocompounds derived from Phyllodium pulchellum, a medicinal plant known for various therapeutic properties. Using in-silico molecular docking, compounds such as Citrusinol, Alpha amyrin, Beta Sitosterol and others were assessed for their interaction with key proinflammatory targets like TNF-α and IFN-α. Among them, Citrusinol demonstrated the highest binding affinity and favorable ADME and drug-likeness properties, suggesting its promise as a potential anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory agent for further research and development. The project helped me understand the interaction between inflammatory cytokines and immune regulators at molecular level and the importance of integrating traditional medicinal knowledge with modern approaches to identify promising therapeutic candidates.

PROJECTS

Targeting BTK: A high-throughput screening and Molecular Dynamics Simulation study to identify potentialdrug candidates by exploring Plant-derived and Repurposed compounds

National Institute of Technology Calicut, India

August 2023 - Current

This computational research project focuses on identifying plant-based and repurposed inhibitors against Bruton's Tyrosine Kinase. The focus was on inhibitors that could act as therapeutic agents against B-cell related diseases such as Rheumatoid Arthritis, Multiple Sclerosis, Autoimmune diseases and Lymphoma. The work involved high throughput screening of databases for potential inhibitors, followed by ADME analysis, protein preparation, molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulation.

Transcriptome-Guided Discovery of Synergistic Phyto-Compound Combinations for Combatting Carbapenem-Resistant Acetobacter baumannii Infections

National Institute of Technology Calicut

January 2024 - Current

The project involves finding plant based compounds which when combined with antibiotics will produce a synergistic effect against the multi-drug resistant bacteria Acinetobacter baumannii. This project was subdivided into Mini Project, Major Project part 1 and Major Project part 2.

Acinetobacter baumannii is a critical priority pathogen known for causing severe nosocomial infections with high mortality rate due to rapidly developing resistance to multiple antibiotics, including carbapenems.

The study focuses on identifying plant based novel therapeutic agents which when combined with antibiotics supresses the multidrug resistant bacteria. The focus was to inhibit Class-D-Beta-Lactamases which are the primary mechanism by which bacteria acquire resistance to drugs

It involved in silico screening of possible plant based inhibitors, followed by ADME analysis, protein preparation, molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulation. This pointed 4-aminocoumarin as the potential drug candidate. 4-aminocoumarin was synthesized in the lab and tested for Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) and Minimum Bactericidal Concentration (MBC) against the bacteria. The compound revealed synergistic activity with one of the carbapenem class antibiotics.

Currently, transcriptome studies to understand the mechanism of the bacteria against the drug candidate is being conducted
